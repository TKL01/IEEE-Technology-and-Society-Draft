	\begin{abstract}
A future robotized society will be characterized by meaningful human–robot interactions, in which robots are seamlessly integrated into daily human activities, augmenting and empowering people to sustain their societal self-determination, participation, and well-being. Currently, however, robots are primarily developed for scripted motions and  subject to significant programming complexity, which constitutes a major barrier to their broader adoption. It remains difficult to verbally instruct a robot to retrieve a previously unseen object or to follow a human operator who dynamically determines the destination of the payload carried by the robot. Conversely, robots rarely communicate their current perception or reasoning about the sensed environment to humans. This limitation prevents humans from understanding the robot’s internal representation of the world and from adjusting their expectations regarding robotic assistance and guidance. To address this challenge, we present the development of a novel infrastructure that integrates semantic object detection and segmentation based on YOLO to construct a scene graph, followed by the interpretation of the structured scene through Multimodal Large Language Models to support inclusive human–robot teaming. This framework enables humans to adapt and communicate their objectives to the robot and receive guidance and assistance from it in a natural way, regardless of their technical background. Experimental evaluations indicate that users perceive the robotic system as demonstrating high performance in terms of reliable communication,   understanding the robot behavior, handling previously unseen objects, perceived safety, and express intention to adopt the system for societal tasks.
	\end{abstract}

	Mobile robots are increasingly programmed to perform daily societal activities, such as room cleaning and payload transportation, thereby relieving humans from monotonous and physically demanding tasks \cite{gona2024intelligent}. Usually, such robots rely on predefined rules and task sequences that govern their behavior. However, as these robots become more deeply integrated into human-populated environments characterized by uncertainties imposed by human behaviors and decisions that constrained tasks, the engineering effort required to adapt robots makes purely static programming impractical. As a result, citizens often struggle to spontaneously instruct robots to  reach and skillfully interact (e.g., safely follow) with entities, such as humans or objects, particularly when these entities are in motion during everyday activities in  unstructured environments.
	
Traditional approaches that address these challenges rely on a Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller to influence the robot that tracks the Cartesian pose of entities identified through stereo image processing. While effective for simple applications, this level of robot intelligence does not support semantic reasoning, limiting the robot ability to adapt to task requirements beyond the scene geometry, previously unseen situations, or to generalize across tasks.

Robotized assistance and guidance can be accessed through natural human language, eliminating the need for users to possess technical expertise. This fosters inclusion and broader adoption. At the same time, the availability of such shared representations enhances the explainability of robotic decision-making, enabling humans to better anticipate and influence robot behavior. \section{Related Work}\label{relatedwork}

Robotized societal applications typically operate in unstructured environments, such as home settings, where scenes exhibit diverse spatial, temporal, and contextual dynamics. These dynamics arise from routine human activities as well as uncertainties caused by human intentions and the appearance and disappearance of entities with varying properties and affordances in the 3D scene \cite{yalcinkaya2024towards}. SGs provide a structured and multimodal representation of the sensed environment, enabling robots to reason about their operational space as well as determine and generalize appropriate actions. Statistical methods, particularly neural-network-based approaches, have therefore been widely adopted for object detection and scene graph generation (SGG) in mobile robotics. The following subsections review relevant methods and outline their connections to our approach.

\subsection{Layered Architecture for Scene Graph}

Mobile robots need to reason over contextualized and structured information in order to plan and achieve goals. Recently, SGs have been adapted for mobile robotics, and multiple layers of abstraction of the real-world scene have been introduced for this purpose. These include the spatial layer (locations within the scene), the semantic layer (the meaning of entities, including their state, affordances, predicates, and other attributes), the topological layer (connectivity between locations), and the task-related layer (robot goals) \cite{rana2023sayplan,im2024egtr}. The entities present in the scene populate these layers, which differ in several respects. One such distinction is the update rate of entity attributes \cite{rana2023sayplan,im2024egtr}. For example, the robot initial and final positions may be known in advance and can therefore be treated as fixed, whereas task completion may be required within a short time window, prompting the robot to execute motions more quickly. In contrast, in object-following applications, the robot desired position changes over time. In this work, we leverage a large language model to introduce an additional maturation layer grounded in the task and interaction history of the mobile robot. As a result, our SG matures through experience.

\subsection{Algorithms for the generation of scene graphs}
Three main classes of algorithms have emerged for the design of SGs, as summarized in \cref{tab:features}. Edge-dual scene graph generation captures multi-object relationships for SGG \cite{kim2025semantic}. The approach combines object detection based on a fast Region-based Convolutional Neural Network (R-CNN), an edge-dual scene graph, and an object-relation-centric Message Passing Neural Network (MPNN). The MPNN aggregates information from neighboring entities to learn object- and relation-centric features, enabling iterative updates of entity representations and producing a feature vector for the entire graph \cite{kim2025semantic,su2024adopting}. Recent SGG approaches also exploit attention mechanisms by connecting objects with significant attention weights derived from the self-attention layers of a pre-trained DEtection TRansformer (DETR) \cite{im2024egtr}. A third strategy is Convolutional Scene Graph Generation (CSGG), where objects are encoded as bounding-box center points and relationships are represented as 2D vector fields pointing from subject to object \cite{liu2021fully}. Key characteristics of these approaches are summarized in \cite{liu2024repsgg} and reported in \cref{tab:features}.  We use a box-based approach (YOLO, CNN) and extend it by employing an MLLM to determine relations between objects. To this end, we combine a deterministic preprocessing (see Fig. \ref{transformation_template}), natural language, and logical inference.


\subsection{Tools for the genereration of scene graphs}

Different tools have been developed to perform the intermediate operations required for SGG from sensed data. An overview of these tools is presented in \cref{tab:scenegraph_technologies}. For example, YOLO and SAM (Segment Anything Model) play key roles in object detection and segmentation tasks. SAM is a general-purpose model capable of performing pixel-level segmentation, whereas YOLO primarily provides bounding-box detection. YOLO is specifically optimized for low latency, making it suitable for real-time scenarios in dynamic, human-centered environments where rapid collision avoidance is critical. This enables faster collision handling while the system follows human motion paths within our framework. CLIP is widely used for extracting semantic features due to its open-vocabulary capabilities. It supports multimodal classification at both the pixel and bounding-box levels. The shared semantic embedding space provided by CLIP enables a seamless transition from image-based perception to language-driven understanding, for example through GPT-based prompting.


\subsection{Operationalization  of scene graphs}
Scene graphs have been used in several societal applications, such as finding seating in indoor environments \cite{ravichandran2025safety}, assisting with cooking \cite{kwon2025large}, and supporting city-scale inspection missions \cite{viswanathan2024xflie,amiri2022reasoning}. However, most SGs are constructed from single images, whereas human activities typically span longer time horizons.

To address long-term goals, \cite{ravichandran2025safety} federates local SGs from successive images into a global SG. In contrast, our approach leverages an MLLM that exploits reasoning history and generalization capabilities for long-term reasoning. Similar contextual grounding is explored in \cite{kwon2025large} using the generative capabilities of an LLM. We extend this scene-centered contextualization by considering internal robotic processes, including motion dynamics and energy consumption. Our framework thereby supports  motion planning and feasibility assessment.


\section{Scene graph}\label{scenegraph}

A scene graph (SG) represents a scene in a structured form by encoding objects, their attributes, and relationships. Scene graph generation (SGG) can be derived from images, text, or videos. An SG is a hierarchical graph consisting of nodes $O$ and edges $E$. Nodes represent objects such as people, animals, places, or items (e.g., bicycle, car), as well as parts of objects (e.g., a person's arm). Each node may include attributes describing the object's state, such as color, size, or pose. Edges represent relationships between object pairs, describing actions (e.g., man holds cup) or spatial relations (e.g., man stands in front of bench). These relationships are typically expressed as triples 
$\langle \text{subject} - \text{predicate} - \text{object} \rangle$ \cite{hli2024sgg}.

\noindent Various models and technologies are used for SGG across robotics, computer vision, autonomous navigation, and human--machine interaction (see Table~\ref{tab:scenegraph_technologies}). Recent advances in Foundation Models have shifted the field from class-limited object recognition toward universal segmentation models (e.g., SAM/SAM2) and open-vocabulary encoders such as CLIP, which provide pixel-accurate masks and semantic embeddings. These capabilities allow modern MLLMs and vision--language models (VLMs) to recognize not only object classes but also semantic states and complex relationships. Frameworks such as ConceptGraphs and 3DGraphLLM leverage this information to construct SGs interpretable for LLMs while grounding objects in 3D space using geometric data from sensors such as LiDAR or depth cameras. Other approaches, including VLMaps and Graph2Nav, translate these spatial relations directly into navigation commands.

\noindent Many existing frameworks require prior mapping or 3D reconstruction \cite{3dgraphllm,hovsg,openin,interaction}, while others construct maps online in previously unknown environments \cite{conceptgraphs,graph2nav,sgnav,lgx,vlmaps}. However, such approaches typically depend on powerful hardware, multiple sensors, and extensive preprocessing, often involving LiDAR or depth-based scanning of entire environments. The resulting maps are then enriched with semantic descriptions, for instance through MLLMs, enabling tasks such as visual question answering about specific objects. In contrast, our approach aims to be resource-efficient and cost-effective. It generates a scene graph directly from the current image only when needed (e.g., to describe the scene), avoiding time-consuming mapping and enabling dynamic operation.

\subsection{Setup and Applications}
The interface provides a selection of different versions and sizes of YOLO and YOLOE (open prompt) models for object detection and segmentation as well as MLLMs for scene interpretation, which can be expanded as needed.  In addition, specific YOLO settings can be configured, such as showing and hiding bounding boxes, classification, and confidence. With an additional confidence slide bar, objects that are not well recognized can be filtered, which are below the set threshold. The dataflow can be divided into mainly five layers (Fig.\ref{framework_architecture}):
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Voice (Speech-To-Text, STT) or text input via the graphical user interface (scene description, navigation goal, robot status or casual chat). 
	\item Object detection with distance. If casual conversation with the MLLM is initiated then this step is skipped. 
	\item Preprocessing via rule-based intent analyzer depending on the input. This step could potentially be replaced with a small LLM for orchestrating the tasks, but a rule-based approach offers lower latency and is more ressource efficient. In dynamic and critical situations, e.g., in road traffic, fast processing is a top priority.
	\item Depending on the intention, the response is output via MLLM or rule-based text (preconfigured response) if the situation requires it. In any case, the implemented text-to-speech system (TTS) reads out the answer. Complex scene descriptions or casual conversations are forwarded to MLLM, while the robot's status and navigation goals (following an object or simply driving to an object) are rule-based processes.    
	\item When navigation or system status is requested, the system writes or subscribes to ROS2 topics to control the robot or query its status.
\end{enumerate}

Efficient YOLO-based object detection serves three purposes in the system. First, YOLO provides real-time 2D detections that are fused with the ZED 2i depth measurements, leveraging the ZED SDK, to estimate spatial object data (e.g., distance and 3D localization, see Fig. \ref{framework_architecture}), which are then formatted (Fig. \ref{transformation_template}) and passed to the MLLM as part of the grounded input prompt. Second, although modern MLLMs/VLMs can also produce 2D bounding boxes, YOLO is used as a lightweight preliminary detection layer to reduce MLLM workload and reduce inference latency.


Third, it is used to navigate and follow a recognized object. To this end, the distance from each detected object to the camera and the pairwise inter-object distances within a frame are computed using the 3D Euclidean distance. The selection of the target object is initiated through its unique identification (ID), which is facilitated by either vocal input or interface selection.

	\label{eq:o1}
\end{equation}
Where the object $o_1$ receives the attributes ID, confidence value, 3D positions, 3D velocity, and the classification \textit{static} if the velocity is zero.
The object data is expressed in the depth camera reference frame, i.e., the estimated 3D position ($x$, $y$, $z$) and the velocity ($v_x$, $v_y$, $v_z$) are relative to the camera.The GUI offers various selections of MLLMs. In the application, a distinction is made between local MLLMs and cloud-based MLLMs accessed via OpenRouter.
Local and offline models (e.g. via Ollama) such as Gemma 3 or Qwen 2.5 VL can be hosted on consumer PCs and edge devices such as the Jetson Orin, depending on the size of the models.
Gemma 3 supports multimodal input with a very large context window and offers features that can improve attention to small details in images. Qwen 2.5 VL is often preferred for accurate, consistent captions, especially when there is text in the image (OCR). Both run locally with many quantization options and are capable of multiple languages \cite{gemma3} \cite{qwen25vl}. This option offers the advantages of control, versioning, and data protection, but requires suitable hardware. Quantized and specialized or trainable models can be used for weaker hardware and since they run locally, internet connection is not required. The second option is online, cloud hosted models via the OpenRouter API. With a single API key, it can connect to many different models from OpenAI, Google, Meta, Anthropic, etc. Most of these are subject to a fee per query and have a request limit. 
Nevertheless, inference takes place on the servers of the respective providers, meaning that significantly less powerful hardware (e.g., GPU) is required. In this case, factors such as server availability and data security are beyond the user's control. In potentially unstructured areas, connection problems may occur. For these reasons, both variants were implemented, which can be selected as needed and depending on internet availability and costs (resource costs and monetary costs). The experiments, in section \ref{sec:experiments}, demonstrate both MLLMs hosted on the local network (on high-performance PCs in the lab) and a MLLM running on the Jetson AGX Orin 32 GB. The Jetson AGX Orin aditionally serves as an onboard computer for the mobile robot, enabling a comparison between onboard MLLMs and fixed MLLMs  running on advanced hardware. For demonstration purposes, the GUI is also installed on the Jetson and on a standard laptop. A client-server architecture (Rest API) was implemented, in which individual images along with the merged object data are sent as a prompt to the server (MLLM host pc) via the GUI. Note that the server receives a single image/frame with speed data to signal to the MLLM whether objects are moving or stationary. Without this information, it would be impossible to determine from a single image whether objects are moving or not. By analyzing a single image instead of multiple frames, resources are efficiently used and do not overload the MLLM with too many requests. The following text-prompt then instructs the MLLM on processing data. The goal is to enable safe navigation for a visually impaired person: 
\noindent
\begin{center} 
	\fcolorbox{black}{black!5}{%
		\begin{minipage}{0.92\columnwidth}
			\setlength{\parskip}{0.5em}
			\ttfamily
			
			Describe the image briefly and precisely using the object data provided.
			
			\{object\_description\}
			
			Describe what can be seen in the image,
			where important objects are located,
			and provide information about possible obstacles.
		\end{minipage}%
	}
\end{center}

The object data, defined as \textit{object\_description} is provided in the prompt and has been translated into concise bullet points during the preprocessing stage. Initially, a template for the raw data was employed. Fig. \ref{transformation_template} presents a schematic representation of the result of this deterministic transformation using the template. By combining the image data with the readable object data using the MLLM, an understandable 3D scene is described, which is accessible to humans. A SG is also constructed by enriching  the existing object data with descriptions from the MLLM, which are both saved in CSV and text files, which then serves as local structured memory for the model. The model can therefore e.g. ask for clarification regarding navigation if ambiguities arise, such as when the user wants to navigate to a specific car but there are multiple cars in the scene. The result of the scene interpretation is displayed as text in the output window of the GUI and can optionally be played back via an audio file generated by activating the text-to-speech function. 
This could be used, for example, to help a blind or visually impaired person navigate. The result of this semantic scene interpretation is shown in section \ref{subsec:sceneunderstanding}.

\section{Evaluation and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}
This section presents hardware test results and benchmarks from experiments with YOLO models within pAIrSEEption and various inference times with MLLMs such as Gemma 3 and Qwen 2.5 VL until a scene description is generated. The second experiment shows results from a usability evaluation of the framework with a focus on human robot interaction and object following.

\subsection{Performance Analysis of YOLO Models}
Fig. \ref{fig:yolo_comparison} shows the average FPS and GPU usage of a variety of popular YOLO models of different sizes that are integrated into pAIrSEEption, respectively. The FPS value is an important measure for evaluating the perception module in the overall system and affects the response time, safety, precision of navigation, and tracking. The framework offers integration into the system of pytorch (\textit{.pt}), \textit{.onnx}, or \textit{.engine} (TensoRT) YOLO model. The \textit{.engine} models that have been optimized with TensorRT (to 16-Bit Floating Point for testing purposes) on a laptop computer (Nvidia RTX A2000 graphics card with 8 GB VRAM, 32 GB RAM and i7-13800H processor) show promising results regarding FPS and GPU usage and demonstrate that the framework runs well even on ordinary consumer laptops.
A confidence threshold of 40\% was selected on the GUI. Data collection took place in a laboratory setting. The hardware performance data of the different YOLO models was recorded on the GUI over 1750 frames with varying activity within the scene (static and moving objects, while moving objects are counted as active). 
The data recording is part of the GUI, and the diagrams were created from this data. The results show that the \textit{yolo11n} (nano) model achieves the best value in the average FPS evaluation with just under 40 FPS, which was to be expected as this model is the smallest in this test series.
The large \textit{yolo11l\_tensorRT} is at a similar level and shows that optimization with TensorRT achieves a significant improvement of approximately 5 FPS on average compared to the standard \textit{yolo11l.pt} model. When comparing the two optimized TensorRT models in the overall system, the YOLO 11 version is ahead of version 8 by around 5 FPS on average and with a negligibly higher GPU consumption of 2\% to 3\%. As expected, segmentation models (with  suffix \textit{seg} in the diagram) typically achieve lower FPS compared to their object detection counterpart models. In future work, this could be used for avoidance of objects, where this pixel mask is used to avoid objects more precisely.

\subsection{Performance of MLLMs and Showcase of Scene Understanding}
\label{subsec:sceneunderstanding}
Fig. \ref{timing_breakdown} shows the duration of various Gemma 3 and Qwen 2.5 VL models to generate and describe a current scene with object data. Ollama is used to determine this  metric and record it in measurement logs. These logs consist of several parts. They contain metadata such as timestamps, an ID (for unique assignment of images and audio files to an analysis log), and the model used for scene interpretation. In addition, the number of input and output tokens is determined. In this test, the same scene was always tested with different models. 
The duration from when the user initiates a request for the scene description to the output of the response is broken down into three parts: the model load time, the input processing and the output generation sent from the server to the consumer laptop running pAIrSEEption. The runtime analysis shows that model size is the decisive factor in the system’s responsiveness. Extremely large models such as the Qwen 2.5 VL 32B, with a processing time of over 60 seconds, are not suitable for assistive systems due to the hardware limitations of the server (i9-13900KF processor, RTX A 5000 graphics card and 128 GB RAM). Smaller variants such as the Husky Qwen 2.5 VL 7B and the Gemma 3 12B, with total durations of less than 6 seconds, offer a good basis for real-time applications. However, when hosted directly on the Jetson platform (see G3 12B q4 Jetson), the initial model load time (model\_load\_time) is the bottleneck and accounts for the majority of the latency. The measurements show that the application-specific Husky customizations via modelfiles do not generate any measurable performance overhead. For operational use, a lightweight 7B or 12B model is generally preferred. In case of the Jetson, to reduce the loading delay between requests, the model can be kept in the RAM. 
Fig. \ref{mllm_scene_lab} shows a scene in the lab described by Gemma 3 12B. The generated output demonstrates a high degree of spatial awareness and semantic coherence. The fusing of YOLO-based object detection with ZED-derived depth data, shows that the model is able to effectively translate metric coordinates into a natural language representation. It chronologically orders the obstacles based on distance (from 1.8 m to 5.0 m) without specific prior instructions. The model identifies detected static objects as potential obstacles, which showcase the successful reasoning within the task-related layer of the SG. The remark about the floor level indicate ability to interpret global scene properties from the visual frame beyond the YOLO preprocessing.
It demonstrates a human-centered approach in which immediate safety and situational awareness of the user are prioritized.
The primary focus here is on local and self-hosted models. It should be noted, that this test was also conducted for larger cloud-based MLLMs via OpenRouter, such as Llama4 Scout/Maverick or Gemini 2, which also provided excellent descriptions of the scene based on the object data.

\subsection{Usability Study}

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup}
Participants (N = 9) from various technical/engineering backgrounds were asked to interact  with the mobile Husky robot (indoors) using voice commands in German (e.g., "Folge Person 1" - "Follow Person 1") after a brief introduction of the GUI and the system. After voice input, the robot should then pursue the corresponding target over a random distance determined by the participants. Voice control can also be used to stop or start a different navigation. The robot should provide audio feedback to the user about its actions. After the interaction, the participants were asked to anonymously evaluate their overall impression in a survey.

\subsubsection{Study Design}
To evaluate the usability of mainly the voice control and object tracking (following an object with the mobile robot), a survey consisting of nine Likert-scale items (with scores of 1 to 5) and three open questions was conducted. The items were structured as follows:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
	\item \textbf{Voice Control:} 
	\begin{enumerate}[label=Q\arabic*:, series=survey, widest=Q12, leftmargin=*]
		\item Ease of giving commands. 
		\item Reliability of instruction recognition. 
		\item Necessity of repetitions (negative item*). 
	\end{enumerate}
	
	\item \textbf{Object Tracking:} 
	\begin{enumerate}[label=Q\arabic*:, resume=survey, widest=Q12, leftmargin=*]
		\item Precise and general object identification.
		\item System response time to desired objectives. 
		\item Frequency of losing the target (negative item*). 
	\end{enumerate}
	
	\item \textbf{User Experience:} 
	\begin{enumerate}[label=Q\arabic*:, resume=survey, widest=Q12, leftmargin=*]
		\item Overall ease of robot understanding and use. 
		\item Perceived safety during operation. 
		\item Intention to use the developed system in real-world applications. 
	\end{enumerate}
	
	\item \textbf{Qualitative Feedback (Open-ended):} 
	\begin{enumerate}[label=Q\arabic*:, resume=survey, widest=Q12, leftmargin=*]
		\item Positive aspects of the system operation. 
		\item Suggestions for improving user experience$\slash$comfort.
		\item Proposed additional functions or commands. 
	\end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}

The questions $Q_{i,i\in \{1,\cdots, 12\}}$ were rated by participants (P1 to P9) on a scale of 1 to 5, with 1 being the lowest/worst rating and 5 being the highest/best rating. For questions Q3 and Q6, negative items were reverse-coded for statistical analysis (1 = lowest/best, 5 = highest/worst). For every question (Q1 to Q9) a mean $\bar{x}$ and a sample standard deviation (SD) $s$ was computed to make a quantitative statement. Since participant P5 skipped question Q3, n = 8 was used to calculate mean and standard derivation for this particular question.

The quantitative results in Table \ref{tab:user_study_results} indicate a high level of user satisfaction across all categories. Particular emphasis was placed on the ease of use of voice commands, the perceived safety during robot operation (mean: $\bar{x}_{Q1, Q8} = 4.67$ and standard derivation (SD): $s_{Q1, Q8} = 0.50$ respectively), the subsequent correct object identification by the system was rated the highest with an average rating of $\bar{x}_{Q4} = 4.78$ and the lowest SD with $s_{Q4} = 0.44$. 
Furthermore, very high ratings for the recognition reliability, the system response time and the overall ease of use ($\bar{x}_{Q2, Q5, Q7} = 4.56$) indicate that the tracking behavior was perceived as predictable and non-threatening. 
The participants are also willing to use such a system in real-world applications ($\bar{x}_{Q9}$ = 4.22). While the low ratings for necessity of (voice) repetitions ($\bar{x}_{Q3)} = 1.75$) and the frequency of losing the tracked target ($\bar{x}_{Q6)} = 1.56$) show that the implementation of voice control and object detection works very well.  
The low SD $s$ across most items indicates a high consensus among participants.
It should be noted, however, that the participants all come from the field of engineering, so that a high level of technical affinity and an intrinsic interest in robotics must be assumed. Therefore, the acceptance to use the system in real-world applications as well as the perceived ease of use might be lower for lay users. The study is also limited by the small sample size ($N=9$), which serves as a pilot evaluation.  
For further investigations, visually impaired individuals should be consulted, for example, to evaluate the system's scene description (Fig. \ref{mllm_scene_lab}) and determine in what ways it can be helpful for independent navigation. In addition, while the current object tracking performance was rated highly, future work should investigate more cluttered/unstructured environments where the probability of losing the target (Q6) might increase.
Given their technical background, participants provided sophisticated qualitative feedback (Q10 to Q12) that highlights both operational performance and technical scalability. They positively emphasized the precision and fast response time of the system. They also praised the easy-to-understand and clear interface and the constant distance maintenance.
Potential for improvement was seen primarily in the robot driving around corners (slow turns) and a broader language support. While the current architecture leverages faster-whisper \cite{fasterwhisper} for automatic language recognition for the voice command inputs, the STT output currently is limited to German. However, the MLLMs used in the system natively support multilingual interaction and it remains a high priority task for future iterations to further foster inclusion. 
Beyond the interaction, interoperability with web-based and industry standards, in particular the Internet of Things and Industrial Internet of Things ecosystems, was also proposed.
Further suggestions were also made for optimizing re-identification (Re-ID) of the object, improving obstacle detection, and providing more detailed instructions for voice commands.
Additional functions suggested included a reverse gear, a voice command for a 180° turn, and the integration of an additional robot arm as well as inter-robot communication. 
Furthermore, participants suggested implementing fallback mechanisms to handle (voice) signal loss and exploring additional communication modalities to ensure reliable operation.
Overall, the results suggest that our system can effectively lower the entry barrier for robot interaction and shows great potential for service robotics in domestic and industrial settings.


\section{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}
This work presents a hybrid approach to generating SGs that combines precise 3D sensor data with the semantic intelligence of multimodal LLMs that controls a mobile robot. By transforming metric object data into natural language description, the environment becomes not only comprehensible to, for example, visually impaired individuals but also directly navigable through the assessment of obstacles. The results show that AI responses remain factually accurate when provided with sensor data. The user survey confirms that the framework is reliable and appealing from the user's perspective. The system makes a contribution to inclusive human-robot interaction by translating complex computer vision data into human-centered, helpful assistance.

\section{Biography Section}
If you have an EPS/PDF photo (graphicx package needed), extra braces are
needed around the contents of the optional argument to biography to prevent
the LaTeX parser from getting confused when it sees the complicated
$\backslash${\tt{includegraphics}} command within an optional argument. (You can create
your own custom macro containing the $\backslash${\tt{includegraphics}} command to make things
simpler here.)

\vspace{11pt}

\bf{If you include a photo:}\vspace{-33pt}
\begin{IEEEbiography}[{\includegraphics[width=1in,height=1.25in,clip,keepaspectratio]{fig1}}]{Michael Shell}
	Use $\backslash${\tt{begin\{IEEEbiography\}}} and then for the 1st argument use $\backslash${\tt{includegraphics}} to declare and link the author photo.
	Use the author name as the 3rd argument followed by the biography text.
\end{IEEEbiography}

\vspace{11pt}

\bf{If you will not include a photo:}\vspace{-33pt}
\begin{IEEEbiographynophoto}{John Doe}
	Use $\backslash${\tt{begin\{IEEEbiographynophoto\}}} and the author name as the argument followed by the biography text.
\end{IEEEbiographynophoto}
